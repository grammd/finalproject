The Values of the Greeks

Published: June 22, 2025 • Author: Dominic Grammatico

The Will to Power is perhaps Nietzsche's most misunderstood concept. It is not, as many have interpreted it, a call for domination over others or a justification for political tyranny. Rather, it is a fundamental drive that Nietzsche sees as the underlying force of all life—the drive to expand, to grow, to overcome obstacles, and to assert oneself in the world.

This will is not limited to human beings, but is present in all living things. It is the force that drives a plant to grow toward the sun, that compels an animal to hunt and survive, that motivates human beings to create, to learn, to achieve. It is the fundamental energy of life itself.

The will to power is not a doctrine of domination or control, but a celebration of life's fundamental energy. It is about recognizing and embracing the creative force that drives all life, and about using this force to create new possibilities for human existence.

It is about saying "yes" to life, about embracing challenges and difficulties as opportunities for growth, about constantly striving to become more than one currently is. It is about the creative will that drives human beings to question, to explore, to create, and to grow.
